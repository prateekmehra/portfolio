Clothing the recycling gap

DEFINITION
Clothing recycling refers to converting clothes into reusable fibers, which is different than donating or reselling clothes.

OVERVIEW

The project aimed at investigating why people don’t recycle their clothes more often. It explored ways to educate people on the benefits of clothing recycling, and design solutions to increase habitual clothing recycling.


MY ROLE

Designer and Prototyper


TEAM

Colleen McDevitt, Phecda Louie, Prateek Mehra and Yeva T.


TIMELINE

8 Weeks


DESIGN QUESTION

How might we help households in King County recycle their clothes with ease and purpose?

Design Process

Why Clothing Recycling?

In King County alone about 95% of clothing articles disposed into landfills could instead be recycled. We believe there is lack of awareness and communication between households, business community, and local governments around recycling clothing.


Why Households?
  • Households are the closests representation of families or group of individuals with typically familiar recycling habits
  • Households did not restrict the age group and background of people we could include in our user base

Secondary Research — Competitive Product Survey & Historical Analysis

We did a literature survey on the subject matter and we got in touch with King County officials and their clothing recycling subsidiary Threadcycle to obtain any existing material/research study conducted within King County.


Online Survey, Interviews and Contextual Inquiries

An online survey helped us distinguish the demographic that is aware of the concept of clothing recycling, those who habitually recycle clothes, and those who aren't aware of it at all. Using the 159 responses from various King County neighborhood Facebook groups, Buy Nothing groups et cetera that we gathered from this survey, we prepared focused interview questions.





We visited the Dearborn Goodwill Outlet on a busy Saturday morning where we conducted 7 contextual inquiries with Goodwill customers (individuals & families) about their awareness and experience of recycling with Goodwill. These inquiries informed our long haul interview questions as well.


Extreme Interviews

Three in-depth interviews of our fellow un-primed (lacking clothing recycling knowledge) international graduate students at the University of Washington; which helped us understand expectations of potential "unaware" consumers of our solution. And another one with an industry insider, at her home.




Empathy Tools and Still Photo Diary

To triangulate our research we explored the Give Back Box recycling process and also got a participant from our survey to do a still photo diary for us while she recycled clothes without any priming about where and how to recycle by us. Look it up here.

Research Insights
The Gap
In the last year itself, 93% of households donated unwanted clothing, while only 20% recycled.




Key Issues
Clothing Recycling Awareness


Location: recycling dropboxes


  • 50% of households did not know they could recycle clothes.
  • 81% of households were unaware of clothing recycling drop boxes within King County.

Want more convenient options


Know what clothing to recycle


  • 77% of households wanted more convenient ways of recycling clothing.
  • 78% of households struggled to identify the types of clothes that could be recycled.

The Discovery

Awareness
Inclusivity
Embarrassment


Many had behaviors of donating clothing, but a lack of awareness was a main reason many had not recycled clothes in the past.


Another subtle but powerful clue concerned the addition of visual aids to the recycling education materials and the recycling bins themselves to clearly convey the message to a wide audience (e.g. non-English speakers, young children yet to learn to read, new King County residents not familiar with recycling, etc.).


Another insight uncovered in our research concerned the stigmas associated with donating or recycling personal items like underwear and old bras. “Weird”, “awkward”, and “embarrassed” were some of the sentiments expressed when discussing these frequently replaced pieces that otherwise would make excellent recycling candidates.


Personas

Our research led us to three different personas. We decided to narrow down our target audience to those who had never recycled since designing a solution for them would also work for households that already recycle their unwanted clothing and are only looking for more convenient ways for doing it.


Our primary target users are those who donate frequently and either do not recycle or recycle selectively. These are households that might be motivated to recycle clothing instead of putting them into the trash.


Our secondary target users are those who infrequently donate and do not recycle. These households are not motivated to dispose of clothing in more conscious ways and thus need to be informed about the consequences of trashing clothing and its impact on the environment. They are unfamiliar with the different ways to donate and recycle clothing.


  
  
Design Goals
We identified five design goals that an ideal solution should achieve. Having these goals was helpful in prioritizing the features during our ideation and brainstorming sessions.

Why Engaging?
The solution should engage the entire household including adults and children and incorporate positive emotions to make recycling more fun, family-centered and community-oriented.
  
Why Educational?
The solution should educate users about the concept of “clothing recycling” as converting clothing into reusable fibers, which is different from donating or reselling clothes. 50% of households were unaware of this fact.
  
Why Inclusive?
The solution should incorporate visual aids to engage a wider audience.
  
Why Convenient?
The solution should be speedy allowing users to get rid of unwanted clothes quickly.
  
Why Action-Oriented?
The solution should help users recycle clothing habitually to keep their carbon-footprint to a minimum.
  
Ideation

We conducted a 6-hour long brainstorming and ideation session where we built an affinity map to try and conquer our problem space. The map was based on our design principles to identify broad themes and narrow down our scope by sketching solutions that would incorporate as many of our design principles as possible.




Through the course of the project, we came up with 211 ideas all over the range from very feasible (ex: a mobile app) to extremely wild (ex: a masquerade party). Look at some of our sketches here.


With a whole bunch of ideas to evaluate, we decided to narrow down to our top 3 ideas using feasibility as one of our criteria. This led us to:

  • A clothing recycling app
  • A purple bin
  • A pamphlet

We decided to go with the mobile application since it was befitting for all our design requirements and our carefully selected design principles. It was also the best medium to reach a wider audience and provide more educational content easily. The features we finalised for the app were:

Inclusive
  • Available in many languages
  • In-built voice features and screen readers for all abilities and preferences
Educational and Engaging
  • Engaging educational content about recycling
  • Features personal recycling goals and landfill statistics
Convenient
  • Seamless unwanted clothing pick up and drop-off arrangement
Action-Oriented
  • Chatbot and guided tour to ensure action by our users
  • Gamification to keep users engaged
Prototyping

We quickly mocked up a low fidelity prototype of our application using InVision Freehand and InVision Studio. We used this low fidelity version to understand how our potential users would use the application.

Usability Testing

7 users tested our lo-fi prototype. Based on these testing sessions, we made 6 major design changes and conducted another 5 usability tests on our next iteration, built with a slightly higher fidelity and incorporating images through InVision Studio.




Key Findings
  • App tour confusing for users
  • Recycle Cart found to be unnecessary
  • Gamification points purpose unclear
  • Desire to know what happens to recycled items
  • Prototype did not cater to international users


High Fidelity Prototype


Improvements from Low Fidelity Prototype
  • Streamlined the overall experience
  • Added an onboarding experience that articulates what “clothing recycling” is as well as the process
  • Replaced the “trip” and “cart” verbiage with more appropriate terminology to avoid misuses of common terms
  • Removed the Recycle Cart experience

Final Design
Finishing Touches

We included an app onboarding experience as many users were confused about what the app is about. We altered gamification from earning points to tracking how many pounds of clothing users are diverting from the landfill. And finally, we introduced localized options that match King County’s most commonly spoken languages.

Impact

We got in touch with the clothing recycling advocates in King County and shared our insights and solutions. We also had a meeting with King County and City of Seattle officials to discuss the project details.


We further publicized the project through Facebook's Design in the Wild contest.

Reflection

Our belief is that technology can be harnessed for social good, and we want to advocate the use of Human Centered Design principles in government and the public sector. This is where we believe the next UX frontier is, it’s where we were inspired to serve, and it’s where this approach could be truly transformational.


If I had more time, I would spend time refining the educational contents of our app, make it more engaging and help bring this much needed application to fill the gap of a stop curated solution for clothing recycling.
